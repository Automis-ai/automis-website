### Why a flat monthly fee beats per-minute billing

The reason a flat fee matters is simple: your phone traffic is lumpy. A single ad, a local event, or a seasonal spike can double your inbound calls in a week. With per-minute pricing, that surge becomes a cost you cannot forecast. With a flat plan, the same surge is just more appointments booked at the same price. Every plan also carries a 30-day performance guarantee, which means you can watch it book real appointments before you commit for the long term.

> Rule: if a vendor bills you per minute, they profit when your callers wait on hold. Flat pricing puts you both on the same side.

### What EUR 297 to 497 per month actually buys

At this price you are not renting a voicemail with a friendlier voice. You are getting a voice agent that greets the caller, answers routine questions, checks a live calendar, books the correct appointment type, writes it back to your CRM, and follows up on the calls that slip through. Put next to a single part-time wage, it is a rounding error, and it works every hour of every day rather than a shift.

## What drives the price of an AI receptionist?

The price is driven by call volume and by how much work the agent does on each call, not by hidden usage meters. On the two Automis tiers, the difference between EUR 297 and EUR 497 per month comes down to the scale of the phone line and the depth of the workflow behind it.

### Call volume and concurrency

A single-line clinic that takes a few dozen calls a day sits comfortably on the Starter plan. A multi-line practice, or a business running paid ads that spike inbound calls, needs the headroom of the higher tier so that two, three, or more callers can be handled at the exact same moment without a busy signal. Concurrency is the quiet cost driver: a human receptionist can only hold one line at a time, and an AI receptionist that can hold many is worth more precisely when you are busiest.

### Depth of the workflow

A basic "answer and take a message" bot is cheap because it does almost nothing. An AI voice receptionist that qualifies the caller, checks a live calendar, books the correct appointment type, and writes it back to your CRM is doing the job you would otherwise pay a person to do. The Automis [Voice AI receptionist](/voice-ai) sits at the second end of that range: it is built to complete the booking, not just log that a call happened.

### Integrations and the recovery layer

Two capabilities quietly separate a real receptionist from a novelty. The first is CRM and calendar integration, so a booked call becomes a real, synced appointment. The second is missed-call recovery: when a call does slip through, the system follows up instead of letting the lead evaporate. Both are included rather than sold as costly add-ons, which is why the headline price stays flat.

> Rule: you are not paying for minutes of talk time. You are paying for booked appointments that show up on your calendar.

## Is an AI receptionist cheaper than hiring a human receptionist?

Yes, and the gap is larger than the salary line alone suggests. A full-time receptionist in the US costs roughly USD 35,000 to 45,000 per year in base pay, an illustrative range drawn from public salary sources such as the [US Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics data](https://www.bls.gov/oes/current/oes434171.htm) and [Glassdoor self-reported salaries](https://www.glassdoor.com/Salaries/receptionist-salary-SRCH_KO0,12.htm); the European equivalent is broadly comparable and varies by country. That figure is only the starting point.

On top of salary you carry payroll taxes, holiday pay, sick days, training, turnover when they leave, and the plain fact that one person covers roughly eight hours on weekdays. Nights, weekends, lunch breaks, and the second call that comes in while they are already on the phone are all uncovered. An AI receptionist at EUR 297 to 497 per month covers all of those hours, answers concurrent calls, and never quits. Even at the top tier that is well under one fifth of a single salary, for coverage a single hire cannot physically provide.

This is not an argument to fire your front desk. A good AI receptionist frees your team from the phone so they can look after the people actually in the room, and it catches the calls your team was never going to reach in the first place.

## What is the real cost of the calls you already miss?

The most expensive receptionist is the one you do not have, because every unanswered call is revenue walking out the door. Between 30 and 50 percent of calls to local businesses go to voicemail. In an appointment-based business, a voicemail is rarely a callback. It is a prospect who dials the next clinic, the next salon, or the next firm on the list.

### A worked example on real numbers

Put a number on it. Say your average new booking is worth EUR 250 in first visit plus early repeat value, and you miss ten calls a week. If even three of those ten would have booked, that is EUR 750 a week, or roughly EUR 3,000 a month, in bookings that never happened. Against a EUR 297 plan, the receptionist has to recover barely more than one of those calls a week to pay for itself, and everything above that line is margin you were previously donating to the competitor who did pick up.

That loss is invisible on your profit and loss statement because it never became an invoice, which is exactly why it goes unmanaged. An AI receptionist priced at a flat few hundred euros a month exists to convert that silent leakage back into booked appointments.
